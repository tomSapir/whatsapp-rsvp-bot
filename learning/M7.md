# M7 — Reminders (in simple words)

## What this stage is

Some guests just don't answer. M7 is the polite nag: a background job that
runs every hour, finds the guests who got an invite and stayed silent, and
re-sends the invite template — within strict limits.

## Who gets a reminder?

A guest must pass **all** of these checks:

1. **Still silent** — their status is `invited`. The moment a guest answers
   (yes or no), their status changes, so they fall out of this list
   automatically. No "unsubscribe" logic needed — the state machine already
   does it.
2. **Actually invited** — guests still in `draft` (added but invite never
   sent) are never chased.
3. **Budget left** — at most `max_count` reminders per guest (configured,
   default 2). Nobody likes a bot that won't stop.
4. **Enough time passed** — at least N days (default 3) since the **last
   touch**: the invite, or the previous reminder if there was one.
5. **The event hasn't happened yet** — the hard cutoff. On or after the
   wedding date, the chase is over, no matter what.

### The subtle bug we designed around (check 4)

The plan says "remind after N days". Naive version: "invited more than N days
ago? send." But the job runs **every hour** — so one hour after the first
reminder, the guest is *still* "invited more than N days ago", and the second
reminder would fire immediately, and the third... the whole budget spent in
one afternoon.

The fix: measure the N days from the **last touch** (`last_reminded_at` if
set, otherwise `invited_at`). Now reminders are spaced N days apart, the way
a human would do it.

## Why a template again?

A reminder goes to someone who has been silent for days — so the 24-hour
free-text window (see M3) is long closed. WhatsApp's rule: outside the
window, only pre-approved **templates** may be sent. So the reminder is a
template, in the guest's language, and each send is also written to the
message log and re-arms the conversation to "waiting for yes/no".

## Deliberate gap (from the plan)

A guest who tapped **Yes** but never said how many people is **not** chased
automatically — only truly silent guests are. The dashboard (M8) will show
those "confirmed but incomplete" guests, and the host nudges them with a
button. Automatic nagging of people who already said yes felt too pushy.

## The clock trick (how this is testable)

The big question of this stage: how do you test "3 days passed" without
waiting 3 days?

Answer: the logic function never looks at a real clock. It receives `now` as
a **parameter**. Production passes the actual time; each test passes whatever
time suits the story it's telling — "one day after the invite" (no reminder),
"five days after" (reminder), "the wedding day" (nothing, forever). This is
the same dependency-injection idea as the fake WhatsApp client and the stub
parser — the clock is just one more dependency you can hand in.

So `app/reminders.py` is split in two:

- `send_due_reminders(...)` — all the logic. Takes the session, the WhatsApp
  client, and `now`. This is what the 14 tests hammer.
- `create_reminder_scheduler(...)` — three lines of APScheduler wiring that
  call the logic function every hour with the *real* clock. M9 will start it
  next to the web server.
